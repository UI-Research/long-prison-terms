POLICIES HOW POLICY DECISIONS KEEP PEOPLE IN PRISON
The steady rise of long prison terms is the result of deliberate policy decisions. The 1970s were marked by rising crime and a growing skepticism that prison could rehabilitate people.
Policymakers shifted their priorities, reimagining prisons as a way to achieve the twin goals of punishing people who commit crimes and removing them from society.
“I did 35 years. I did 35 years, 10 months, 19 days, and 11 hours to be exact. Straight through. No prerelease, no work release, nothing. Straight through…. There's a lot of people that I've come in contact with that didn't believe that they would keep somebody in prison 35 years.
There's people that got out with me that had 40 years in. The public is so unaware of what actually goes on.”

At the same time, there was emerging concern that indeterminate sentencing systems, which gave judges and parole boards a great deal of discretion, were contributing to leniency and racially disparate outcomes. These forces paved the way for a wave of “tough on crime” policies that favored rigid, certain, and severe punishment. Capitalizing on the public’s growing fear of violent crime and on tensions caused by social change, policymakers campaigned on platforms that highlighted crime and public safety as a major political issue nationwide. In the 1980s and 90s, lawmakers on both sides of the aisle enacted policies that swept more people into the criminal justice system and held them there longer, despite scarce evidence that this would help control crime.

The pursuit of increasingly punitive sentencing policies eclipsed other goals of the justice system, like proportionality and parsimony—in other words, that the punishment should fit the crime and should be no stricter than necessary. The modern era of sentencing may have been born from a desire for greater consistency and certainty, but it came to be defined mainly by its severity.

The strategy of trying to get crime prevention by locking people up for very long periods of time is not well-founded. It never was. Never had any empirical basis, but it had an intuitive basis at a time when many people felt that we were facing a zombie apocalypse of crime where unchangingly scary, bad, high-rate offenders would be in our midst unless we did something about it.
Jonathan Simon, UC Berkeley School of Law

States enacted these punitive changes at the front and back ends of the criminal justice system. On the front end—when people first enter the system—policy changes made sentences longer for a variety of crimes. Many states adopted determinate sentencing schemes that established fixed penalties and left judges powerless to consider individual circumstances. Tougher punishments undermined the important goals of proportionality and parsimony in sentencing, allowing people to be punished severely for even low-level crimes.

On the back end are release policies that affect people already in prison and how long they’ll stay. Many states increased the minimum amount of a sentence people must serve and removed or restricted release options, like parole. By erasing opportunities to earn an earlier release, these policies removed incentives for people to undergo the transformative personal growth that actually prevents reoffending.

Policymakers pursued their tough-on-crime agenda by enacting laws—many still on the books today— that handed down long sentences for many offenses. Some states changed their laws to make offenses eligible for prison time that had previously been punished with community supervision. Others changed their sentencing thresholds to lower the quantity of drugs or the value of stolen property required to trigger longer sentences.

Often, the sentences allowed under these new laws were disproportionately long compared with the severity of the crime. In some states, broad sentencing ranges put into effect for certain offenses give judges considerable freedom to hand out harsh punishments. In Utah, for instance, first-degree felonies carry a minimum sentence of 5 years—and a maximum of life. In Louisiana, the penalty for armed robbery can range from 5 to 99 years.

Experts have suggested that longer sentences became the norm for all types of offenses partly because of a growing acceptance of severe sentences for the most serious crimes. In our criminal justice system, these sentences have set a historically unprecedented standard of punishment that has normalized harsh penalties for all other offenses.

Popular “bumper-sticker” crime policies drove up time served for certain types of crimes.

Certain crimes or circumstances can automatically trigger mandatory sentence increases if, for example, an offense is gang-related or if it’s committed near a school. These rules can require judges to hand out longer sentences, even if they don’t think those sentences are warranted. Furthermore, these sentencing enhancements tended to disproportionately affect communities of color, exacerbating racial disparities.

As crime and public safety became more of a kitchen-table issue, lawmakers promoted “tough on crime” policies directly to voters. They appealed to the public with memorable slogans, like “Three strikes and you’re out,” “Seven deadly sins,” or “Truth in Sentencing.”

Case study: Florida

Florida enacted a “10-20-Life” law in 1999 that triggered long mandatory sentences for people convicted of certain crimes involving a firearm. Simply displaying a gun could earn someone 10 years; firing it, even as a warning shot, could earn them 20. Judges were not allowed to make exceptions.

Even though the number of new people sentenced every year under this enhancement stayed fairly steady, the number of people in prison serving 10-20-Life sentences skyrocketed because so few were released each year. As new people entered, this group continued to stack up.

Although Florida policymakers recently reformed the 10-20-Life law to give judges more discretion in sentencing, about one in ten people still in Florida prisons today—nearly 11,000 people—was sentenced under this law.

Many states adopted mandatory minimum sentences for people who have prior convictions, such as “Three Strikes” laws.

Spurred by high-profile crimes committed by people who had been released from prison, calls for harsher punishments for repeat offending ushered in a wave of legislation that ratcheted up penalties for subsequent convictions. Convinced that dangerous people were cycling in and out of prison, the public embraced these changes despite a lack of evidence that such laws would help reduce crime.

Case study: California

Though it was not the first state to adopt such a policy, California’s notorious Three Strikes law stands out as one of the most extreme. Enacted in 1994, the law doubled the penalty for a second felony if the first was violent, and it triggered a sentence of 25 years to life for any third felony—even petty theft.

A 2012 ballot initiative (Proposition 36) reformed the law to prevent less serious crimes from triggering a third-strike life sentence and included a retroactivity clause that made more than 3,000 people in California prisons eligible to petition for reduced sentences. It did not, however, reform the law for people with more serious third-strike offenses, including some drug crimes— nor did it change the second-strike provision.

Today, “second-strikers” continue to receive long sentences. Even while reforms to reduce overcrowding between 2006 and 2016 caused California’s total prison population to drop by 25 percent, the number of people in prison serving a second-strike sentence has held steady.

These mandatory sentencing laws have caused a major shift in the make-up of California’s prison population. In 2006, one in five people had been sentenced under the two-strikes provision. Today, second-strikers make up a full quarter of the state’s prison population.

California was not the only state to adopt these types of mandatory minimums. States like Georgia, Tennessee, and South Carolina also have extreme “Two Strikes” policies that require mandatory life sentences without the possibility of parole for certain second offenses.

Historically, people in prison could get time off their sentences for maintaining clean disciplinary records or participating in programs. Parole boards traditionally weighed these factors and other signs of personal transformation when deciding who to release. These credits were integral to state sentencing structures. In fact, when judges made sentencing decisions, they took into account that people could earn time off.

A wave of legislation in the 1980s and 90s, however, limited or eliminated these “good time” or “earned time” credits. Many states began to adopt determinate sentencing structures and “truth in sentencing” laws that required people to serve a set percentage of their minimum sentences, regardless of their behavior in prison. In states that embraced determinate sentencing, the amount of time people spent in prison was suddenly dictated at the moment of sentencing, leaving little flexibility to account for their personal growth over the years spent in prison. By the turn of the century, 41 states and the District of Columbia had adopted some form of truth in sentencing law.

“By the time I went to go see the parole board… in 2007, we didn’t have any more education. We didn’t have any more programs. We were warehousing prisoners.”
MONICA JAHNER

Stripped of the opportunity to work toward their own release, people serving long sentences were given little structural incentive to take part in programming, develop skills, or otherwise try to transform their lives. Instead of serving as centers of rehabilitation, prisons became warehouses where people served time with few opportunities to redeem themselves and little hope for the future.

Case study: Michigan

Michigan’s truth in sentencing law is one of the most extreme, requiring people to serve 100 percent of their minimum sentences in prison. The 1998 law barred people convicted of certain serious offenses from earning credits to reduce their time in prison. By 2000, that rule applied to everyone entering Michigan prisons.

These changes drove up the time served by people who could otherwise have earned credits for good conduct. Michigan’s stringent release policies have helped make it one of the states with the longest average time served.

Meanwhile, by the turn of the century, 21 states had abolished or seriously restricted parole eligibility for many offenses. Parole boards, in the states where they were retained, became less likely to release people who had served their minimum sentences. Newly punitive attitudes and widespread skepticism about the possibility of rehabilitating people in prison meant that many people—especially those with the most serious convictions—found parole beyond their reach, no matter what they did.

“I was given a 6 to life and I wound up doing 25 years. I went to the parole board after that first six years and thereafter, I went another eight times before they finally released me.”

Even the opportunity to appear before a parole board for consideration became less likely. Statutory changes in some states extended the amount of time people had to wait for their next hearing after being denied parole. As the size and makeup of parole boards changed, so did the prospects for parole-eligible people to have their cases heard. Because most board members are selected by state governors and legislatures, the composition and behavior of a board can shift quickly based on political considerations, leading to marked changes in parole rates over time that have nothing to do with the people being considered for parole.

Case study: Michigan

Michigan’s history demonstrates just how dramatically parole practices can fluctuate.

In recent decades, the number of people paroled in Michigan each year has varied in response to political decisions about release policies and procedures. In 1992, the state overhauled the parole-granting system and changed the board makeup from civil servants to political appointees. In just five years, parole approvals dropped by 10 percent, and the number of people who stayed in prison longer than their court-imposed sentence nearly doubled.

In 2009, Governor Jennifer Granholm signed an executive order expanding the parole board to increase its capacity to review cases that were being continually deferred. Within the year, the number of paroles granted skyrocketed. When the following governor Rick Snyder took office in 2011, he reinstated the former parole board.

Parole policies and practices are often extremely reactive, responding quickly to incidents that grab the public’s attention. High-profile crimes committed by people on parole raise the perceived political stakes of granting parole to others. Risk-averse parole board members may simply prefer not to grant parole rather than take a chance that someone they release will reoffend.

There was a real fear of rising levels of violence, sometimes crystallized in very spectacular and highly publicized incidents that weren’t necessarily normative in any sense but captured for people a sense that they were living in a society that had lost its basic control over violence.

Case study: Pennsylvania and Arkansas

Pennsylvania’s decline in commutations for life-sentenced people in the 1990s was prompted in part by the headline-grabbing crimes committed by one person released on parole. In Arkansas, a similar incident led to a sharp rise in parole revocations and a spike in the prison population.

In some cases, though, states have avoided these consequences by taking a more measured response to such incidents. Although Pennsylvania initially responded to another murder in 2008 by instating a moratorium on parole, the state then conducted a study of its parole policies. After determining that they were still largely safe and effective, the state reinstated parole the next year. During the brief moratorium, the state’s prison population swelled.

The use of life sentences rose sharply, especially life without the possibility of parole.

The number of people serving life sentences has more than quadrupled since 1984, and it rose most sharply for those with no possibility of parole. As of 2016, one in every nine people in prison was serving a life sentence. When virtual life sentences—those that exceed a person’s likely lifespan—are included, that figure increases to one in seven.

Before the 1970s, few served the entirety of a “life” sentence, and life without the possibility of parole existed in only seven states. Now, every state except Alaska has adopted it. Meanwhile, parole for life sentences has become much less likely. And, over the past few decades, more and more life sentences have been given to people convicted of crimes other than murder and even for nonviolent offenses.

This rapid shift reflected a modern sentencing reality that favored indefinite imprisonment over rehabilitation. After the US Supreme Court temporarily banned the death penalty in 1972, the popularity of life without parole sentences exploded as states sought alternatives to capital punishment. Today, the United States remains one of only a few countries that issues sentences of life without parole.

Case study: Pennsylvania

No one with a life sentence in Pennsylvania is eligible for parole. As one of the first states to adopt sentences of life without the possibility of parole, Pennsylvania has the second-largest life without parole population in the country. One in ten people serving this sentence in any state prison is incarcerated in Pennsylvania.

Before 1980, Pennsylvania governors often commuted life sentences to 20 years, but this practice has slowed nearly to a halt in recent decades. Since 1980, the number of people in Pennsylvania serving sentences longer than 10 years grew twice as quickly as for people serving sentences of 10 years or less. Over time, people with sentences longer than a decade steadily grew from 15 to 27 percent of Pennsylvania’s prison population.

These policy decisions have had serious consequences for people, families, and entire communities.

Today, many states continue to uphold these policies and practices, even though the ensuing decades have shown “tough on crime”-era policy to be largely ineffective—and even counterproductive—for accomplishing public safety goals.

It has taken years for the consequences of these punitive policies to fully manifest at the state level, expanding prison populations and straining state budgets as people serving long prison terms stack up. But those personally affected by these policies felt the effects immediately—and have lived with them for decades. People serving long prison terms have watched their lives pass by, while their families and communities have felt their absence.
